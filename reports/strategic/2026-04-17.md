## 3. Promising Leads

1. **[Determination of Turning Radius and Lateral Acceleration by GNSS/INS Sensor](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/22/6/2298/pdf)** — Provides ground-truth lateral-acceleration models (RMSE 0.016 g) for vehicles ≤3.5 t, directly usable for calibrating cornering thresholds by vehicle weight class.

2. **[Driving Behavior Risk Measurement and Cluster Analysis](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/13/9/5675/pdf)** — Offers a complete, reproducible composite scoring pipeline (CRITIC weighting + IQR thresholds + K-means) with validated four-class output and expert-aligned labels — the closest published analog to the composite formula we need.

3. **[Age and gender differences in time to collision at braking](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25307380/)** — Quantifies demographic-driven braking behavior at scale (527K brake events), directly informing adaptive threshold design and reducing nuisance alerts for different driver cohorts.

4. **[Study of Loads Acting on the Load during the Sudden Braking of a Vehicle](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/13/3/1559/pdf)** — Rare empirical data on how vehicle category and pavement type change deceleration forces on cargo, bridging the Safety Thresholds and Cost/Cargo Damage pillars.

5. **[An exploration of active safety technologies for commercial vehicles](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41966718/)** — Demonstrates multi-source risk-level clustering with SHAP interpretability for commercial buses, providing a deployable template for fleet-level risk segmentation and transparent alerting.

## 4. Overall Strategic Position

We are approximately **75–80% of the way** to having a sufficient research foundation to design a first-generation fleet telematics scoring and reporting platform; the behavioral metrics and scoring architecture is essentially evidence-complete, and contextual enrichment is well-supported. **The single biggest blocker** is the absence of vehicle-category-specific G-force threshold tables with validated detection performance — without these, any scoring engine risks systematic mis-classification across mixed fleets (cars, vans, trucks), and the cost/maintenance pillar cannot be credibly linked to event severity. Secondarily, the near-total absence of empirical data linking harsh-event counts to specific maintenance cost items (brake wear, tire wear, cargo damage) means the ROI case for fleet managers rests almost entirely on fuel savings and crash reduction, leaving a significant value-communication gap.

## 6. What Would You Build?

### Ready Now

1. **Multi-source driver behavior scoring engine with CRITIC-weighted composite scores.** The evidence base for this is deep and converging. Use the CRITIC-weighting + IQR-threshold approach from [Driving Behavior Risk Measurement and Cluster Analysis](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/13/9/5675/pdf) as the structural template. Input features: hard-braking frequency, hard-acceleration frequency, harsh-cornering frequency, speeding duration/frequency, and jerk variability — all normalized per 1,000 km of exposure and segmented by road type and time of day as per [Potential GPS-Observed Driving Behavior Exposure Metrics](https://trid.trb.org/view/1289262). For CAN-equipped vehicles, add throttle aggressiveness, RPM patterns, and brake-pedal force features as per [Driving behavior analysis and classification by vehicle OBD data](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1007/s11227-023-05364-3). Output: a 0–100 driver score with four risk tiers (conservative, safe, aggressive, dangerous). Apply vehicle-mass normalization per [Telematics Truths – Orion](https://orionfi.com/telematics-truths/) even if the full category-specific lookup table is not yet available — a simple mass-correction coefficient is better than none.

2. **Context-enriched alerting pipeline with adaptive thresholds.** Implement road-type and time-of-day context overlays on all harsh-event detection, as multiple studies confirm these contextual factors are the strongest moderators of risk ([Combining accelerometer data and contextual variables](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.trf.2016.06.006); [Quantifying the effect of roadway, driver, vehicle, and location characteristics](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34455341/)). Use load-condition detection for trucks ([Driving style identification among truck drivers](https://doi.org/10.1080/19439962.2023.2233089)) and weather data integration to widen thresholds in rain ([Normal and risky driving patterns in clear and rainy weather](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.iatssr.2020.07.002)). Cross-check accelerometer events with engine RPM to reduce false alerts ([Piritiz](https://piritiz.com/gps-detects-speeding-harsh-driving-2/)).

3. **Nudge-based coaching system with personalized feedback strategy.** The field experiment from [Nudging Drivers to Safety](https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.2021.4063) provides a ready-made design: personal-best nudges for high-performing drivers, personal-average nudges for low-performers. Pair with scorecards and gamification elements per [Tourmo AI](https://www.tourmo.ai/resources/learn/identifying-the-riskiest-driver-behaviors-to-improve-fleet-safety-tm) and BBS feedback per [Effects of Behavior-Based Driver Feedback Systems](https://www.mdpi.com/2313-576X/10/1/24/pdf). Stage-of-change tailoring from [GPS-enabled smart phone App](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.trpro.2017.05.154) adds another personalization axis. Monitor eco-driving decay and trigger refresher coaching when drift is detected ([Using on-board logging devices for eco-driving](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.trd.2009.05.009)).

### What to Defer

- **Vehicle-category-specific threshold tables:** The absence of a validated lookup table by vehicle class means deploying hard-coded G-force thresholds for mixed fleets (especially HGV vs. car) carries unquantified false-positive/negative risk. Use configurable defaults with fleet-level calibration for now; defer a "definitive" threshold product until dedicated empirical testing or a comprehensive published table is found.
- **Maintenance cost attribution models:** There is insufficient peer-reviewed evidence linking specific harsh-event types/frequencies to brake wear, tire wear, or suspension costs. Defer building a "cost-per-harsh-event" calculator or maintenance-impact report until empirical data is sourced; in the interim, limit ROI claims to fuel savings (~10–30%) and crash reduction.
- **Cultural/national driving pattern adjustments:** Evidence is anecdotal. Defer building country-specific scoring baselines until cross-country comparative studies are identified.

### Biggest Insight

**Data quality and sampling rate have a larger effect on risk classification than the choice of scoring algorithm.** Geotab's controlled experiment ([Putting fleet data quality to test](https://www.geotab.com/white-paper/fleet-data-quality/)) demonstrated that lower-frequency or gap-ridden telemetry can flip high-risk drivers into low-risk classifications and vice versa — meaning that investing in reliable, high-frequency data ingestion with event-triggered capture is more impactful than refining the statistical model applied to noisy data. This should directly shape hardware procurement requirements, data pipeline SLAs, and how the platform communicates confidence in its own scores.
