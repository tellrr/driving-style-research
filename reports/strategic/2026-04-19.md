## 4. Overall Strategic Position

We are approximately **80% of the way** to having sufficient research to design and ship a V1 fleet telematics service suite. The behavior scoring, fatigue detection, eco-driving, and alerting pillars are well-supported with both academic and commercial evidence, and we can begin building immediately. **The single biggest blocker is the absence of peer-reviewed, vehicle-category-specific G-force threshold tables with validated false-positive rates** — without this, our thresholds will be empirically set and require field validation rather than being scientifically pre-calibrated, which adds time-to-confidence for customers. A secondary blocker is the lack of dose-response data linking event severity to specific maintenance cost outcomes, which weakens the "total cost of ownership" value proposition beyond directional estimates.

## 6. What Would You Build?

### Ready Now

1. **Real-Time Harsh-Event Alerting with AI Nudges.** The evidence is robust: real-time nudges yield a 31% reduction in preventable accidents and 44% drop in near-misses ([From Detect and Repair to Predict and Prevent](https://ijeret.org/index.php/ijeret/article/download/408/389)), smartphone-based feedback reduces speeding and hard braking ([AAF Randomized Field Trial](https://aaafoundation.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/202504-AAAFTS-App-Feedback.pdf)), and feedback + financial incentives outperform feedback alone ([The effect of telematic based feedback and financial…](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0001457521003092)). **Build a configurable threshold-triggered alert system with in-cab audio/visual nudges and a fleet-manager escalation pathway.** Use the Controltech 5–6 km/h/s deceleration target as the default "good" threshold and 8 km/h/s as "harsh," with overrides per vehicle class.

2. **Composite Driver Score (0–100) with Trip-Level Breakdown.** The academic foundation is complete: GPS-derived indices validated at fleet scale ([Driver behavior indices from large-scale fleet telematics…](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0001457522003141)), OBD-II classification at 99.48% accuracy ([Driver Behaviour Classification…OBD-II](https://semarakilmu.com.my/journals/index.php/applied_sciences_eng_tech/article/download/5714/13003)), smartphone-only at 97% ([DriveCheck](https://www.etasr.com/index.php/ETASR/article/download/15030/6285)), HMM-based actuarial scoring ([HMM-Based Telematics Risk Scoring](https://burning-cost.github.io/2026/03/13/insurance-telematics/)), and commercially validated 0–100 formats ([Building Trust with Data – Damoov](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/building-trust-data-mobile-telematics-driver-scores-car-shornikov-ttipf)). **Build a weighted composite score incorporating speed compliance, harsh braking rate, harsh acceleration rate, cornering severity, and fatigue proxy (steering entropy).** Use Bayesian updating with a neutral prior for new drivers, converging after ~500 km of driving (estimate from HMM literature).

3. **Eco-Driving Score + Fuel Waste Estimator.** Evidence is converged: aggressive driving costs 15–40% in fuel economy ([AFDC](https://afdc.energy.gov/conserve/driving-behavior); [Zubie](https://zubie.com/blog/impact-harsh-braking-acceleration-on-fleet/)), eco-driving training yields 6–12% savings ([Evaluation of Eco-Driving Training](https://www.mdpi.com/2071-1050/10/11/3891/pdf?version=1540527035); [Development and Field Trial of a Driver Assistance System](https://purehost.bath.ac.uk/ws/files/19584202/T_ITS_12_06_0249_pdf.pdf)), and Colombian heavy-truck data attributes ~40% of unnecessary fuel use to acceleration/deceleration patterns ([Eco-driving key factors…Colombian case](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.trd.2017.08.012)). **Build a per-trip and per-driver fuel waste estimator that converts harsh events and idling time into liters/dollars wasted, alongside a 0–100 eco-score.**

### What to Defer

- **Precise maintenance cost-per-event calculator.** While directional evidence is strong (driving style ≈ 50% of ownership cost; 20% brake/tire wear reduction from behavioral improvement), we lack the dose-response data to credibly say "each harsh braking event costs €X in brake wear." Ship a qualitative "maintenance impact" indicator but defer quantified €/event claims until we have peer-reviewed data or run our own fleet study.

- **Vehicle-category-specific threshold auto-calibration.** We know a single threshold is suboptimal (highway vs. urban differs; car vs. HGV differs), but without published sensitivity/specificity by vehicle class, we cannot pre-ship scientifically calibrated per-category thresholds. Instead, ship configurable thresholds with sensible defaults and plan a field calibration study.

- **Road-surface quality scoring correction.** The Nairobi study proves the need is real, but a single case study is insufficient to build a generalizable correction algorithm. Defer until IRI overlay data or a multi-geography study is available.

### Biggest Insight

**Professional drivers are equally susceptible to fatigue as nonprofessional drivers** ([Deterioration in Driving Performance During Sleep Deprivation…](https://doi.org/10.1080/15389588.2013.800637)). This overturns a widespread industry assumption and has a direct design implication: fatigue monitoring features (steering entropy analysis, hours-of-service tracking, CAS-style alertness scoring) must be applied at full sensitivity to all drivers regardless of experience level. Any "experienced driver exception" in alerting logic would be scientifically unjustified and dangerous.
