## 4. Overall Strategic Position

We are approximately **80% of the way** to having sufficient research to design and launch a differentiated fleet telematics product. The behavior-scoring and driver-context pillars are effectively actionable now, and the service-design pillar has enough commercial benchmarking to begin implementation. **The single biggest blocker is the absence of peer-reviewed, vehicle-category-specific G-force thresholds with validated false-positive rates** — without this, we must either run our own instrumented test program or default to configurable thresholds with conservative defaults, risking either excessive false alarms (undermining trust) or under-detection of genuinely dangerous events. A secondary blocker is the thin evidence linking telematics-measured harsh events to specific mechanical cost outcomes (brake, suspension, cargo), which limits our ability to deliver the highest-value ROI reports to fleet managers.

## 6. What Would You Build?

### Ready Now

1. **Composite Driver Safety Score with Context-Dependent Event Weighting.** The research base is sufficient to build a weighted composite score using hard braking, hard acceleration, harsh cornering, speeding, and speed variability — with differential weighting by road type (highway vs. urban), as validated by the NYC 4,000-vehicle study ([Driver behavior indices...](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0001457522003141)). The Poisson-Gamma updating framework from the wavelet-based actuarial model ([Portfolio-Anchored Telematics Risk Scoring with Wavelets](https://burning-cost.github.io/2026/03/26/wavelet-telematics-risk-index-lee-badescu-lin-2026/)) provides a principled cold-start mechanism where the score improves with each trip and converges to a stable estimate. The Azuga benchmark (10-point increase → 57% accident reduction) provides a calibration anchor for score interpretation.

2. **Automated Coaching Pipeline with Gamification.** Evidence is overwhelming that behavior-based feedback significantly reduces risky driving: 73.9% reduction in speeding from automated web-based instruction ([Reducing risky driving...](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31847985/)), 76% reduction in harsh acceleration from ADAS + coaching ([On the impact of ADAS...](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36696744/)), and sustained engagement through gamification and scorecards (multiple industry sources). Build trip-level Eco Scores (following the Teltonika six-event model), weekly driver leaderboards, and threshold-triggered coaching modules. Positive reinforcement outperforms punitive approaches ([Tackling Aggressive Driving in the Trucking Industry](https://www.freightstarexpedited.com/post/tackling-aggressive-driving-in-the-trucking-industry-strategies-for-safer-roads)).

3. **Tire Wear Projection Report (CAN-equipped vehicles).** For fleets with CAN-bus access, the virtual tire wear sensor approach is implementable today using wheel speed, RPM, steering angle, vertical load, and braking intensity ([Tire Wear Virtual Sensor](https://www.easyrain.it/tire-wear-without-a-sensor-how-virtual-detection-is-reshaping-vehicle-safety/)). Combined with the validated sensitivity analysis showing braking/acceleration as primary wear drivers ([Tyre Wear Model: Validation and Sensitivity Analysis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/225676180_Tyre_Wear_Model_Validation_and_Sensitivity_Analysis)), a projected-tire-replacement-date report is feasible. For GPS-only vehicles, a cruder proxy based on harsh-event counts per 1,000 km is defensible.

### What to Defer

- **Vehicle-category-specific G-force alert thresholds** — While we can ship configurable thresholds with sensible defaults (e.g., 0.4g braking for passenger cars, 0.3g for HGVs based on physics first principles and the i-DREAMS STZ concept), we cannot yet claim peer-reviewed, validated defaults with known false-positive rates. Defer marketing these as "scientifically calibrated" until instrumented validation or the right publication is found.

- **Brake pad/suspension wear cost projections** — The qualitative link is established (harsh events → accelerated wear), but no quantitative model exists mapping event frequency to replacement intervals or cost. Defer building a "predicted brake maintenance cost" report until empirical data is available; instead, offer a qualitative risk indicator.

- **Country/culture-adjusted scoring baselines** — The cross-cultural evidence is rich (>15 papers this cycle), but translating it into actionable per-country score adjustments requires either proprietary fleet data or a published baseline calibration study. Defer hard-coded cultural adjustments; instead, let the Bayesian updating framework self-calibrate to each fleet's own population distribution.

### Biggest Insight

**Hard braking and hard acceleration are not interchangeable hazard proxies — their predictive power for crash risk swaps depending on road type.** The large-scale NYC study ([Driver behavior indices...](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0001457522003141)) demonstrates that hard braking is the dominant crash correlate on highways, while hard acceleration is the stronger predictor on urban roads. This means a single "harsh event" counter conflating both types will be suboptimal. **The scoring engine must weight braking and acceleration events differentially based on the road context where they occur.** This is a non-obvious design requirement that most commercial systems currently ignore.
